Basic Rules

Introduction

Triheart is a tabletop role-playing game played with pencils, paper, and dice, with friends or acquaintances around a table. Triheart allows the players to tell stories of the adventures of heroes in a fantasy setting.

The Players

Players each play one character within the fantasy world of Triheart. They work together with other players to succeed in challenges they face to achieve great things. The main concern of the players is to be part of a fun and exciting experience for everyone concerned. How well a player does is not measured in the wealth they acquire or power they wield in the game, but in how memorable the characters they play are, and how much they contribute to the enjoyment of the game.

The Game Master

The game master, or GM for short, is played by just one player. The game master is in charge of running everything about the world other than the characters the players are playing. The GM is in charge of describing the world and playing all characters nor being played by other players. The GM is the storyteller and also the final arbitrator of rules presented in this document if there is disagreement among the players. The GM’s job is still for everyone to have fun, and a good GM will not only go by the book but is willing to go with what the table thinks if that will result in everyone having a good time.

Dice

The game uses standard polyhedral dice, available from all good gaming stores and online. The dice are referred to as d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, and d20 after how many sides each kind of die has.

A d10 you have might have numbers from 0 to 9, rather than 1 to 10. With these dice the 0 is read as a 10 instead.

Characters

A character in Triheart is any being who has agency. This means all humanoids, animals, and other beings who are capable of making meaningful decisions regarding the story, no matter how small the being.

Attributes

Every character has a number of attributes, that represent various capabilities of their person. Attributes are represented as number values where ±0 is the average person. Generally, people vary from -5 to +5, but values lower and higher are possible under the right circumstances.

Here are the 6 attributes in Triheart:

Strength

Sheer physical strength. A character’s ability to use melee weapons, jumping, climbing, moving heavy objects, and other feats of athleticism.

Agility

Nimbleness, reflexes, and accuracy. A character’s ability to use ranged weaponry such as bows, perform acrobatics, sneak around, and dodge incoming attacks.

Vitality

A character’s ability to withstand injury, poisons, and illness. Determines a character’s health.

Knowledge

Memory and knowledge. A character’s ability to memorise, recall, and the breadth and depth of their general knowledge.

Instinct

Mind and perception. A character’s ability to hear the feintest of sounds, notice the smallest facial expressions, ability to make split second decisions, and the willpower to stay clam under pressure.

Charisma

Social skills and communication. A character’s ability to make themselves be understood, to impress, and to make others believe.

Note that a character’s charisma has nothing to do with how they look, but with the force of their character in social situations.

Skills

Each attribute has associated with it a wide variety of skills. A skill is a more specific application of an attribute that can be trained. A character can have 0–3 levels in in a particular skill, from not trained at all to expertise. Here follow all of the skills and which attribute they are associated with.

Strength

  • Athletics — How much you can lift, push, pull, and perform other such feats of athleticism
  • Climb — How difficult a surface you can climb and how fast

Agility

  • Acrobatics — How well you can balance on a tightrope, a slippery surface, catch hold of ledges, and perform flips and other acrobatic moves
  • Dexterity — Lockpicking, disarming traps, pickpocketing, everything that requires a great amount of fine motor skills.
  • Stealth — How quietly and sneakily you can move

Vitality – Vitality has no skills associated with it

Knowledge

  • Medicine — How much you know about anatomy and performing first aid
  • Alchemy — How much you know about chemistry and potions
  • Occult — How much you know about spells, magical items, cults, religions, and supernatural beings
  • Nature — How much you know about plants, animals, and surviving in the wilds
  • Forgery — How much you know about official paperwork and how to forge fakes of them along with signatures, seals, and other features

Instinct

  • Perception — How well you are able to notice things through your senses
  • Investigation — How good you are at finding items difficult to spot and examining your surroundings
  • Hunting — How well you are able to locate and track animals in the wilderness
  • Animal Handling — How well you are able to handle and control non-humanoid animals

Charisma

  • Persuasion — How well you can negotiate, barter, and perform diplomacy
  • Deception — How well you can lie, mislead, or disguise yourself
  • Intimidation — How well you can threaten someone
  • Entertainment — How well you can impress a crowd, sing, tell jokes, or dance
  • Streetwise — How well you get along with the common folk on the street, and are able to gather clues and rumours

Checks

Checks are made whenever a player character attempts to do something that is not guaranteed to succeed, such as picking a lock or hitting someone with a sword.

A check is a d20 roll made against a target number (TN). Checks made by character’s use a specific attribute that is then added to the roll. A check is a success if the rolled number is equal or greater than the TN.

When a player wishes to do something, the GM will tell them what kind of a check they will have to make, and what TN it will be made against. If a player also wishes to know what the consequences of failing are, they can ask the GM.

Skill Checks

Often, specific skills will be used to make a check. These checks are known as skill checks.

When making a skill check, you add 5 to your roll per level of skill. For example, if a character with an Agility of 3 and two levels of skill in Dexterity attempts to pick a lock they will roll d20 + 3 + 10.

Combat

The Basics of Combat

Combat is broken down into rounds of combat, usually referred to as rounds.

During a round every side of the battle takes a turn one after another.

During a turn every character on that side may do various things such as cast spells, swing swords, and drink potions.

Characters on the same side act simultaneously or in any order they wish. For example, during a turn a character can first do something, then wait for another character to act, and then do something else.

The Start of Combat

At the start of combat, the GM decides which side of the battle acts first. Usually, this will be the side initiating combat, the first to draw their weapons or charge forward.

If no side is acting before the others, all sides choose one character among them to make an agility roll. The order of combat is defined by these rolls, with the sides with higher rolls acting first. If multiple characters roll the same value, they roll between themselves to determine the order.

The Round and the Turn

During a round every character may take an action, a minor action, and they may move up to their movement. Additionally, all characters can talk freely, within reason. A character may also declare that they would like to take a delayed turn and act out of turn order.

Types of Combat

Combat in can be played out in one of two ways: quick combat and tactical combat.

Quick combat is played similarly to normal gameplay with the GM describing the situation and the players telling the GM what their characters wish to do.

Tactical combat is played using a grid with tokens and follows a much more comprehensive ruleset.

Generally, only relatively short and simple battles should be played using quick combat. A battle with many characters or a lot of complexity is hard to keep track of without the use of tokens and a grid. Tactical combat is not necessary for something simple like beating up one or two guards, if anything having to set up the grid and tokens for such a battle would slow the game down.

The rules do work whichever method of running combat you decide on, and you can play using only one of the systems, or without any combat at all.

If you choose not to use tactical combat, you ought to avoid having large fights, and instead have the game mainly be focused on things other things such as exploration, investigation, or social interaction.

Range

Manoeuvres, spells, and other abilities list a range at which they can be used. Range is noted as the number of tiles from you to the target. A range of 1 tile means that the target must be adjacent, 2 tiles that there is one tile between you and the target.

You can think of 1 tile as being approximate to 1 metre.

When a range is noted in tiles, it is accompanied by one of the following ranges: self, melee, close, short, long, and distant. The ranges are always used outside of tactical combat for quick combat, chases, and general adventuring. Close, short, long, and distant are shortened to C, S, L, and D.

0 tiles (Only the character itself)

A range can either be the exact range they need to be in, such as “2 tiles (C)” meaning the target cannot be in melee range but exactly 2 tiles away in short range or as a range within two distances such as “Melee – 6 tiles (S)” meaning any character between melee and short range, 1 to 6 tiles.

When in quick combat, describe distance using these ranges, e.g. “One of the guards is in short range and the two others are in long range.”  This will make it easier for the players to know which of their abilities can be used effectively on which targets.

Direction

In quick combat there is an infinite amount of directions, just like in the physical world.

In tactical combat there are 8 main directions, which are used when a spell or manoeuvre asks for a direction.

In some rare cases, you may have to use additional directions for up and down, and between those and the main directions.

Movement

Every turn characters may move up to their movement, which for players is 10 tiles. You may move diagonally at no additional cost.

You do not need to use all of your movement at once. You may move, take an action, move, take a minor action, move, as long as your total movement does not exceed your movement.

You cannot end your move on a tile that is already occupied by another character, however you may move through another character if they agree to it.

Jumping and climbing:

You can jump a number of tiles equal to your strength or 2, whichever is higher.

Every tile of movement going vertically up costs an additional point of movement. Similarly, jumping a tile upwards costs 2 tiles of movement.

Falling down does not cost any movement.

Descending, for example climbing down a ladder, costs 1 movement per tile.

In quick combat:

A character moves one range unit at a time, moving from long range to short, close to melee, etc.

Attacks

Attacks are a special kind of check, which most of the time require you to make a roll using a d20 against the targets agility or instinct.

Attacks are usually made when using a spell, manoeuvre, or a basic attack, but other cases such as the usage of magical items can still ask for you to make an attack. When an ability mentions attacks, it means all attacks, not just basic attacks.

Physical attacks:

All attacks that are made by hitting someone with a part of their body or another object. For example, unarmed attacks, attacks made with melee weapons, spell attacks that use touch.

Ranged attacks:

All attacks that are made by hitting someone with a missile that is shot, or a weapon that is thrown. For example, a fireball or lightning bolt spell, shooting someone with an arrow, or throwing an axe.

Spell attacks:

All attacks that are a part of a spell. Spell attacks are almost always also a physical or a ranged attack. Only attacks made as a part of a spell are spell attacks. If a spell mind controls a character to attack someone, that is not a spell attack, since it was not part of a spell, only caused by one

Basic Attacks

A basic attack is an attack that is made without a manoeuvre, a spell, or similar ability.

To make a basic attack against a target, roll 1d20 + Ability Score against the target’s defence. If your roll matches or exceeds it, you hit.

Unless otherwise specified, all ranged attacks use agility, and all physical attacks use strength.

Attacks of Opportunity

Doing certain things in melee range of another character may allow them to do an attack of opportunity.

An attack of opportunity is a melee basic attack. You may take it with any melee weapon you are carrying or make an unarmed or improvised attack.

Things that provoke attacks of opportunity:

  • Casting a spell or performing a manoeuvre that states that it provokes attacks of opportunity
  • Using a basic attack with a two-handed ranged weapon
  • Performing first aid action
  • Moving out of melee range from another creature
  • Moving within the melee range of another creature

Note that this only applies to moving, not shifting.

Attacks of opportunity happen before the action that provoked them is done. If a character tries to move away from your melee range and your attack of opportunity makes them fall unconscious, their movement is cancelled, and they stay within melee range. If a character attempts a two-handed ranged weapon attack and your attack of opportunity kills them, the ranged attack is not made.

Shifting

Shifting is movement that does not provoke attacks of opportunity. A character cannot shift unless a manoeuvre, spell, or other ability specifically allows them to do so.

Defence

As a player, when attacked you will make a defence check. Unless it is otherwise specified, you make defence rolls using your agility. Some abilities allow you to defend using other attributes instead, and some attacks are made against attributes other than what you defend with.

Being Prone

Melee attacks against prone targets gain advantage.

While prone, every tile of movement costs an additional point of movement.

You may get up from being prone as a minor action.

On your turn you may fall prone for free as a part of your move.

Being prone may allow you to use cover more effectively, gaining cover in places where a person standing would not gain it.

Being prone behind cover grants an additional level of advantage on top of what the cover already provides.

Difficult Terrain

Moving a tile over or through difficult terrain, such as a tar covered floor, or notably thick vegetation costs an additional point of movement per tile.

If the terrain is remarkably difficult to move through it may even cost more points of movement per tile.

If a spell or an effect is used to turn tiles that are already difficult terrain into difficult terrain the GM will rule whether or not the combination of the effect and the terrain in question would make the difficult terrain cost even more movement.

In tactical combat, difficult terrain should always be marked on the grid.

Cover

When attacking a target in cover, you gain disadvantage on your attack check.

When defending against an attack while in cover, you gain advantage on your defence check.

Cover only protects from the sides it realistically would, and characters can attempt to find an angle of attack that negates the cover.

In rare cases the GM may rule that cover gives multiple levels of advantage and disadvantage. For example, if you are almost fully behind cover, the GM may rule that your defence gains double advantage.

In tactical combat, cover that grants multiple levels of advantage, is good to have marked on the grid.

Actions

Using an action, you can cast spells and perform manoeuvres with the time of “1 action”, use a basic attack, or do one of the following things:

  • Grapple
  • Shove
  • Help
  • Perform first aid
  • Gain an additional minor action
  • Do a situational action

Grapple

As an action, you can attempt to grapple a character in melee range. The grappler contests their strength against the targets strength or agility, their choice.

While grappling a target:

  • You gain advantage on all melee attacks against the target.
  • One of your hands is occupied by the grappling of the target, meaning you cannot use two-handed weapons.
  • On your turn, you can choose to stop grappling the target.
  • You can use your movement to shift the grappled target within melee range.
  • You can move as long as you don’t leave melee range of the target.
  • You can use your movement to move yourself and shift the target at the same time as long as you stay in melee range.

While being grappled:

  • All attacks against you gain advantage.
  • You cannot use two-handed weapons.
  • You cannot move.
  • As a minor action, you may attempt to escape the grapple. To do so contest your agility against the grappler’s strength.
  • As a minor action, you may attempt to escape the grapple and grapple the grappler. To do so contest your strength against their strength.

If the grappled character or the grappler is shifted, the grappled can choose to escape the grapple.

Two characters can never grapple each other at the same time.

A character can grapple multiple different targets at the same time.

A character can be grappled by many grapplers at once.

Shove

As an action, you can shift a character or an object in melee range back a number of tiles equal to your strength, or none if your strength is negative. You can only shove characters and objects that are of such a size and weight that your character could realistically push them.

Help

You can grant advantage on one check to another character in melee range. You can only help with checks your character could actually help with. For example, distracting someone to make it easier for your friend to pickpocket them, reaching out to help them climb, or smacking away a foe’s shield or weapon to help your friend attack.

Perform First Aid

As an action, you can heal a bloodied character in melee range. If the healing would raise the character’s health to above half of their maximum health, they are instead healed to half of their health + 1.

You cannot perform first aid on yourself.

Performing first aid provokes attacks of opportunity.

The amount of healing done increases with levels of skill in Medicine (KNO).

Gaining an Additional Minor Action

You may use your action to instead take an additional minor action of your choice this turn.

Situational Actions

During your turn you may want to do something that does not fit into any of the predefined actions, such as searching over a body, intimidating foes, or searching for hidden switches.

If the action would realistically take around 6 seconds or less, and your character could do it, you may do it as a situational action.

The GM will then rule what kind of a check, if any, is to be rolled to determine your success.

Minor Actions

Using a minor action, you can cast spells and perform manoeuvres with the time of “1 Minor Action” or do one of the following things:

  • Using items and consumables
  • Do a situational minor action
  • Get up from being prone

Using Items and Consumables

You may drink one potion or use one magical item that takes on minor action to use, or use other similar items, for example a torch to light a bonfire.

Alternatively, you may also have an agreeing character in melee range drink a potion from you.

Situational Minor Actions

During your turn you may want to do something that does not fit into any of the predefined minor actions, such as pressing a button, searching a single pocket of a corpse, or wiping your hand clean of noxious slime.

If the action would realistically take around 2 seconds or less, and your character could do it, you may do it as a situational minor action.

The GM will then rule what kind of a check, if any, is to be rolled to determine your success. Situational minor actions rarely require checks, since most things that would be difficult enough to warrant one are proper actions, and not minor actions.

Get up from being prone

As a minor action, you may get up from being prone.

Delayed Turns

A character may disrupt the regular flow of combat by declaring that they would like to take a delayed turn. This allows you to take your turn in the middle of another side’s turn.

Doing so means you cannot move, take your action or minor action, or do anything else on your actual turn.

Before doing anything on your turn, declare that you would like to take a delayed turn and specify what will be the trigger for it.

The trigger is a single sentence, which describes a specific circumstance upon which you will start your delayed turn.

A delayed turn declaration might sound something like “I would like to take a delayed turn when a hostile character goes through the doorway closest to me.”

The trigger in this declaration is “A hostile character goes through the doorway closes to me.”

If this circumstance happens before it would be your turn again, you may then at that moment interrupt the ongoing turn and take your own. The hostile character will stop just after completing the circumstance of the trigger, in this case moving through the doorway.

After you take your delayed turn, the round continues from where it left off.

If the declared trigger does not come up before it’s your turn again, you will have wasted your turn.

Also note that the GM is aware of what your trigger is and is allowed to play around it, for example, avoiding triggering it (although a friendly GM shouldn’t make it their life goal to avoid all triggers).

The Game Master is allowed to take delayed turns but should do so sparingly to not overcomplicate combat.
